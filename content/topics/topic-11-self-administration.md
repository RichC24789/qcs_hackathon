# Topic 11: Self-administration of medication — risk assessment, MAR charts

**Theme:** Medication
**Primary format:** Text card
**Secondary format:** Quick reference card

---

## Hook

Some service users can and should manage their own medication — but only with a risk assessment in place and the right support around them.

---

## Key points

- Self-administration means the service user manages their own medication independently or with minimal prompting
- A self-administration risk assessment must be completed before a service user takes on this responsibility
- The risk assessment should consider capacity, physical ability, understanding of the medication, and safe storage
- A MAR chart is still required even when a service user self-administers — it documents what should be taken and when
- The arrangement should be reviewed regularly and whenever the service user's health or circumstances change

---

## Quick decision prompt

- **Service user wants to self-administer but no risk assessment exists?** → Do not agree to it yet — speak to your manager to arrange an assessment
- **Service user's health has declined and you are concerned about self-administration?** → Raise with your manager — a review of the risk assessment is needed
- **Service user is self-administering but you notice medication is not being taken?** → Document your concern and report to your manager and GP

---

## Common mistakes

- Assuming a service user with capacity can automatically self-administer without a formal assessment
- Failing to review the arrangement after a hospital admission or change in health
- Not maintaining a MAR chart because "they do it themselves"

---

## Who to tell

- **Your manager** — before any self-administration arrangement begins and if any concerns arise
- **GP** — if the arrangement needs to change or if medication is not being taken as prescribed
