# Topic 29: Wound and skin conditions — observation, escalation, documentation

**Theme:** Infection control
**Primary format:** Text card
**Secondary format:** Scenario quiz

---

## Hook

Noticing a change in someone's skin early and recording it clearly is one of the most important things you can do to prevent a minor issue becoming a serious one.

---

## Key points

- Check skin condition during personal care and record any changes — redness, bruising, breaks, swelling, or discharge
- Use a body map to record the location of any skin concern accurately
- Do not attempt to treat wounds beyond your competency — report and refer
- Pressure areas need particular attention — bony prominences such as heels, hips, and the base of the spine
- Any unexplained bruising or injury must also be considered from a safeguarding perspective

---

## Quick decision prompt

- **New redness over a bony area that does not fade when pressed?** → This may be a grade 1 pressure ulcer — report to your manager and the GP or district nurse immediately
- **Wound appears infected (increased redness, warmth, discharge, odour, or the person has a fever)?** → Report to your manager and contact the GP — do not wait for the next scheduled visit
- **Unexplained bruising or skin injury?** → Document it carefully with a body map and report to your manager — consider whether a safeguarding referral is needed

---

## Common mistakes

- Not documenting a skin concern because it looks minor — early documentation is essential for tracking deterioration
- Repositioning a service user without checking the skin first
- Assuming all bruising is accidental

---

## Who to tell

- **Your manager** — for any new or changing skin condition
- **GP or district nurse** — for wounds, pressure area concerns, or signs of infection
- **Safeguarding lead** — for unexplained injuries

---

## Quiz notes

Scenarios: redness on heel that does not fade; discharge from a leg wound; unexplained bruise on upper arm. Ask what action to take in each case.
