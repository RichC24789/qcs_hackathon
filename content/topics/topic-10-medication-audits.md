# Topic 10: Medication audits — how to conduct, frequency, forms

**Theme:** Medication
**Primary format:** Quick reference card
**Secondary format:** Text card

---

## Hook

Regular medication audits are not just a compliance exercise — they catch errors before they become serious incidents.

---

## Key points

- Medication audits should be completed at least monthly in most care settings
- An audit checks that MAR charts are accurate and up to date, stock counts match records, storage conditions are correct, and competencies are current
- Audits should be completed by a manager or designated senior staff member — not the person who administers the medication day to day
- Any issues found in an audit should be actioned promptly and documented
- Audit results should feed into supervision and team meetings — not just sit in a folder

---

## Quick decision prompt

- **MAR chart has unexplained gaps?** → Investigate before the audit is closed — speak to the staff member involved and record the outcome
- **Stock count does not match the MAR chart?** → This is a serious finding — report to your manager immediately and follow controlled drugs procedures if relevant
- **Audit reveals a pattern of the same type of error?** → This needs a systemic response — training, supervision, or a process change, not just a note on the audit form

---

## Common mistakes

- Treating the audit as a tick-box exercise without following up on findings
- Only auditing when CQC is expected — audits should be routine and scheduled
- Not dating and signing the completed audit form

---

## Who to tell

- **Your manager** — all audit findings, especially anything significant
- **CQC** — serious findings may need to be notified as incidents
- **Staff team** — learning from audits should be shared in team meetings

---

## Quick reference card notes

Checklist format covering the key areas of a monthly medication audit: MAR charts, stock counts, storage, fridge temperatures, competency records, PRN protocols. One page, printable.
