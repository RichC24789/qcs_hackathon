# Topic 24: Head injuries following a fall — observation, escalation

**Theme:** Falls & incidents
**Primary format:** Poster
**Secondary format:** Animation

---

## Hook

A head injury can look minor but become serious hours later — knowing what to watch for can save a life.

---

## Key points

- After any head injury, monitor the person closely for at least 24 hours even if they seem fine
- Warning signs requiring immediate 999 call: loss of consciousness, seizure, repeated vomiting, severe headache, confusion, unequal pupils, weakness in limbs, or difficulty speaking
- People on blood-thinning medication (warfarin, apixaban, rivaroxaban) are at significantly higher risk after any head injury — call 999 regardless of how minor it appears
- Document the injury, the time it occurred, and all observations made during monitoring
- Do not give the person anything to eat or drink until they have been assessed if a serious injury is suspected

---

## Quick decision prompt

- **Person lost consciousness, even briefly?** → Call 999 — do not wait
- **Person is on anticoagulants and has hit their head?** → Call 999 regardless of how they seem
- **Person seems fine but hit their head?** → Carry out 24-hour observation, record every check, and call 999 immediately if any warning sign develops

---

## Common mistakes

- Assuming that because the person is conscious and talking they are fine
- Not knowing whether the person is on blood-thinning medication before deciding whether to call 999
- Not recording observation checks — if something changes later you need a documented baseline

---

## Who to tell

- **999** — for any of the warning signs listed above, or any anticoagulant user who has hit their head
- **Your manager** — immediately after any head injury
- **GP** — if the person is not sent to hospital but you remain concerned
