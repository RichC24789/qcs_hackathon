TOPIC: Controlled drugs
DURATION TARGET: 4–5 minutes
ELEVENLABS VOICE SUGGESTION: Speaker A — younger female, warm and slightly anxious ("Aria"), Speaker B — calm senior female, steady and reassuring ("Bella")

[SPEAKER A]: Priya, can I run something past you before you go? At handover just now, me and Dawn did the controlled drugs count, and the running balance says there should be twelve tablets of the morphine for Mr Okonkwo. But we only counted eleven. I keep thinking it's me. Did I do something wrong?

[SPEAKER B]: First of all, I'm really glad you came straight to me. You did exactly the right thing by counting at handover and not just signing it off. That's the whole point of the count. Let's slow down and take it step by step. You stopped before anyone administered anything, yes?

[SPEAKER A]: Yes. We didn't give anything. Dawn said we should just give the dose and sort the number out after, but it didn't feel right.

[SPEAKER B]: Your instinct was spot on. We never administer a controlled drug when the balance doesn't add up. The stock has to be confirmed before we proceed, every time. If it's wrong, we stop, and we tell the manager. So you've already done the hard part.

[SPEAKER A]: That's the bit I find hard, though. It's one tablet. It feels dramatic to ring someone over one tablet.

[SPEAKER B]: I understand why it feels that way. But one tablet of a controlled drug is exactly the kind of thing we never let slide. The rules around these medicines are stricter for a reason, and a discrepancy gets reported to the manager straight away. Not at the end of the shift. Now. While we're talking, we don't fix the number ourselves and we don't cross anything out in the register either.

[SPEAKER A]: Wait, why can't we just correct it? If we find the tablet, surely we write the right number in.

[SPEAKER B]: Good question. The controlled drugs register is a legal record, so we never alter or cross out an entry. If there's an error, we put the wrong entry in brackets, add a note, and two of us sign and date it. But first the manager investigates. Usually it's an honest recording slip, a dose given but signed in the wrong place, or a miscount when the new box came in. We check back through the entries together.

[SPEAKER A]: So what happens to me and Dawn right now?

[SPEAKER B]: Right now, you keep the cabinet locked, you leave the stock exactly as it is, and you write down what you've found, with both your names. I'll ring the manager and she'll investigate immediately. You're not in trouble. Spotting this is you doing your job well, not badly.

[SPEAKER A]: Okay. That helps. Can I ask one more thing while you're here? When we do give a dose normally, it's two of us, isn't it? I want to be sure I've got that right.

[SPEAKER B]: Yes. Two trained people for every administration. One gives it, one witnesses the whole thing from start to finish. Then both of you sign the register, and you update the running balance there and then. You sign the MAR chart too. Never just one signature on a controlled drug.

[SPEAKER A]: And the storage. It lives in that fixed metal cabinet, separate from the other meds?

[SPEAKER B]: That's right. A locked cabinet, fixed to the wall, used only for controlled drugs, kept locked at all times. Keys go from the person in charge to the next person in charge at handover, never left lying about. And one last thing while it's fresh: we never dispose of a controlled drug ourselves. If Mr Okonkwo no longer needs it, we contact the GP and the pharmacist. Disposal is a witnessed process. Care staff can't just bin it.

[SPEAKER A]: That's really clear. Thank you. I was bracing myself for a telling-off.

[SPEAKER B]: Never. You stopped, you counted, you didn't administer, and you came to me. That's textbook. Let me make that call now.

---
KEY TAKEAWAYS (for show notes / text card pairing):
1. If the controlled drugs count doesn't add up, stop. Don't administer, don't fix the number yourself, and report to your manager immediately — not at the end of the shift.
2. Every controlled drug needs two trained people: one to administer, one to witness the whole process, and both sign the register and update the running balance, plus sign the MAR chart.
3. Store controlled drugs in a locked, fixed cabinet separate from other medicines, and never dispose of them alone — disposal is a witnessed process, so contact the GP and pharmacist.
