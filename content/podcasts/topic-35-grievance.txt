TOPIC: Grievance procedure
DURATION TARGET: 5 minutes
ELEVENLABS VOICE SUGGESTION: Speaker A — frustrated but measured female (e.g. "Aria"), Speaker B — calm, practical male (e.g. "Marcus")

[SPEAKER A]: Can I talk to you about something that's been getting me down? My shift leader keeps giving me the worst shifts, talks over me in handover, and last week took credit for something I sorted out. I don't want to be dramatic, but it's wearing me down. I don't know if I'm overreacting.

[SPEAKER B]: You're not being dramatic — how you're treated at work matters, and you don't have to just put up with it. What you're describing is exactly what a grievance is for. A grievance is just a formal way of raising a concern about your treatment, a colleague, or your working conditions.

[SPEAKER A]: "Grievance" sounds so heavy, though. Like I'm starting a war.

[SPEAKER B]: It doesn't have to be. The first step is usually informal. Often a calm conversation — sometimes with a manager helping — sorts things out without anything formal at all. Would you feel able to raise it directly, or would that feel too difficult?

[SPEAKER A]: Honestly, with this person, I don't think a quiet word would land well.

[SPEAKER B]: That's okay. Then you put it in writing as a formal grievance, to your manager or HR. And because your concern is partly about your shift leader, you'd send it to their manager or to HR — not to them.

[SPEAKER A]: What do I actually put in it?

[SPEAKER B]: Specifics. Not "they're always unfair," but dated examples — "On this date, in handover, this happened." The thing people most often get wrong is keeping it all in their head. Start a personal record now: dates, what happened, who was there, any messages. It makes your case much stronger and easier to follow up.

[SPEAKER A]: Some of it honestly feels like it tips into bullying. Does that change things?

[SPEAKER B]: It follows the same formal process, but it makes documenting specific incidents even more important — dates, what was said, who saw it. And don't downplay it to yourself. If it feels like bullying, raise it clearly rather than softening it.

[SPEAKER A]: That helps — I tend to talk myself out of it.

[SPEAKER B]: A lot of people do. Writing it down helps you see the pattern for what it is.

[SPEAKER A]: And then what — does anything actually happen?

[SPEAKER B]: Yes. Once you submit it, your employer should acknowledge it promptly and hold a hearing within a reasonable time. You have the right to be accompanied at that hearing — a colleague, or a trade union rep if you're a member. And if you're not happy with the outcome, you can appeal in writing.

[SPEAKER A]: I didn't know I could bring someone. That makes it feel less lonely.

[SPEAKER B]: You're not meant to face it alone. There's also ACAS — they give free, independent advice, on 0300 123 1100 — if you want to talk it through with someone outside the organisation first.

[SPEAKER A]: What if I just leave it and hope it settles down?

[SPEAKER B]: You can, but in my experience these things are much easier to deal with while they're current. Leave it six months and the details blur and it's harder to act on. If it's affecting you now, it's worth raising now.

[SPEAKER A]: Okay. I think I'll start by writing down what's already happened, then decide between an informal chat and a formal grievance.

[SPEAKER B]: That's a sensible plan. Get it documented, choose your route, and remember you can bring someone with you. And keep talking to me — you don't have to carry this on your own.

[SPEAKER A]: Thank you. That helps a lot.

---
KEY TAKEAWAYS (for show notes / text card pairing):
1. A grievance is a formal way to raise concerns about your treatment — try the informal route first where you can.
2. Put it in writing with specific, dated examples, and keep your own record of incidents.
3. You can be accompanied at a hearing and appeal the outcome — and ACAS (0300 123 1100) offers free advice.
