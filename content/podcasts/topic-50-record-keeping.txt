TOPIC: Record keeping and documentation
DURATION TARGET: 4–5 minutes
ELEVENLABS VOICE SUGGESTION: Speaker A — "Aria" (warm, slightly anxious female), Speaker B — "Marcus" (calm, reassuring male)

[SPEAKER A]: Can I run something past you? I'm a bit worried and I don't really know what to do. Yesterday I forgot to write up that I'd helped Mr Harmon with his lunch. I noticed this morning, and my shift lead said just go back, slot it into yesterday's notes, and put yesterday's time on it so the record looks complete. That didn't sit right with me, but she's senior, so I said I'd ask.

[SPEAKER B]: First of all, I'm glad you came to me. That instinct you've got, that little feeling something's off, that's exactly the instinct good record keepers have. So let's talk it through. The short answer is no, you shouldn't put yesterday's time on it. A record has to show the real time and date you actually write it. Writing today but dating it yesterday, that's backdating, and we never do that.

[SPEAKER A]: But it's true though. I really did help him. I'm not making anything up. So what's the harm?

[SPEAKER B]: I know it's true, and that matters. But records aren't just about what happened, they're about when it was written and who can trust them. The moment we change a time or slot something in to make the page look tidy, the whole record stops being something anyone can rely on. Records have to be factual, accurate, and never falsified or altered to mislead. Even with good intentions, backdating crosses that line.

[SPEAKER A]: Okay. So what do I actually do? I can't just leave it blank, because then it looks like he didn't get lunch.

[SPEAKER B]: You're right, and you don't leave it blank. You make the entry today, with today's real date and time, and you write something like, "Adding a note about yesterday's lunch, which I forgot to record at the time." Then you describe what happened. You sign it, print your name, and date it. Anyone reading it later sees the truth: care was given yesterday, the note was added today, honestly and openly. That's a strong record, not a weak one.

[SPEAKER A]: That actually feels better. While I've got you, what about when I make a mistake in the writing itself? Last week I wrote the wrong room number and I just scribbled it out really hard.

[SPEAKER B]: Common one. On paper, you never scribble it out so it can't be read, and you never use correction fluid. You draw a single line through the mistake, so the original is still readable underneath, write the correct information next to it, then initial it and date it. The wrong sheet stays in the file too, you don't pull it out. The idea is that nothing is ever hidden. On digital systems it's the same spirit, you amend properly, you don't quietly overwrite.

[SPEAKER A]: Got it. One more thing. Mr Harmon's son asked if he could read his dad's notes. Can I just show him?

[SPEAKER B]: Hold on there. Records are confidential and protected by data protection law. The service user themselves has the right to see their own records, but a family member is different. Before you share anything with anyone else, check with your manager and make sure that person has a proper right to see it. When in doubt, don't share, ask first.

[SPEAKER A]: That makes sense. So basically, write it now, write it true, and if I get something wrong, fix it openly.

[SPEAKER B]: That's it exactly. Write as close to the time as you can, keep it factual and legible, sign and date everything, and never change the past to make it look neater. You've handled this really well. Go and write that note properly today, and you've got nothing to worry about.

---
KEY TAKEAWAYS (for show notes / text card pairing):
1. Records must be factual, accurate, legible, signed and dated, and written as soon after the event as possible — never backdate, falsify, or alter a record to mislead.
2. Correct a genuine mistake by drawing a single line through it, writing the correction alongside, then initialling and dating it — never use correction fluid, scribble it out, or remove the sheet.
3. Records are confidential and covered by data protection — the service user can see their own records, but check with your manager before sharing with anyone else.
