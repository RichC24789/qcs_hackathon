TOPIC: Safeguarding children
DURATION TARGET: 4–5 minutes
ELEVENLABS VOICE SUGGESTION: Speaker A — concerned female (e.g. "Aria"), Speaker B — calm, reassuring male (e.g. "Marcus")

[SPEAKER A]: This one's outside my comfort zone. I work in adult care, but yesterday I was visiting a client and her young grandson was there — about six. He seemed really withdrawn, his clothes weren't right for the weather, and he flinched when his mum raised her voice. I'm not a children's worker. Is it even my place to do anything?

[SPEAKER B]: It absolutely is. This is something a lot of people get wrong — they think children's safeguarding only matters if you work in children's services. It applies anywhere a child might be, including someone's home on an adult visit. If you have a concern about a child, you have a duty to act.

[SPEAKER A]: But I've got no evidence anything's wrong. He didn't say anything to me.

[SPEAKER B]: You don't need evidence. The threshold for children is lower than most people think — a reasonable concern is enough. Withdrawn, not dressed for the weather, flinching — that's reasonable concern. You don't have to prove anything or work out what's happening.

[SPEAKER A]: So what do I do with it?

[SPEAKER B]: You report it to our designated safeguarding lead, straight away. They'll make the referral to children's social care if it's needed. Your job is to pass the concern on, not to investigate it yourself.

[SPEAKER A]: And if he'd actually said something to me — told me someone had hurt him?

[SPEAKER B]: Then you listen, calmly. Don't ask leading questions — don't say "did so-and-so do this?" Let him tell you, reassure him that he's done the right thing telling you, and don't promise to keep it secret. Then report it the same day, and write down what he said in his own words as soon as you can.

[SPEAKER A]: What if I thought he was in danger right then, in the moment?

[SPEAKER B]: If a child is in immediate danger, you call 999 first — before anything else — and then your manager. Don't wait, don't second-guess it.

[SPEAKER A]: Part of me thought I should wait a week, see if it's a one-off.

[SPEAKER B]: That's a common instinct, and I'd gently push back on it. Waiting to see if it resolves is how concerns slip through. If it turns out to be nothing, no harm done — but if it's something, you've acted early. A child can't be the one who has to wait.

[SPEAKER A]: And he's six — that definitely counts as a child?

[SPEAKER B]: Anyone under eighteen is a child. So yes, absolutely.

[SPEAKER A]: I suppose part of me worried I'd cause trouble for the family over nothing.

[SPEAKER B]: I understand that worry. But think of it the other way round: if you stay quiet and something is wrong, that child has no one speaking up. Reporting isn't accusing anyone — it's making sure the right people take a look. And if you ever can't reach our safeguarding lead, there's the NSPCC helpline — 0808 800 5000 — and they'll guide you.

[SPEAKER A]: That's good to know. Do I need to know who our safeguarding lead is, then?

[SPEAKER B]: Yes — that's the one thing I'd ask you to sort out today. Knowing exactly who to go to means you act fast instead of hunting around in a moment that matters. Shall we check the name together now, and then write up what you saw?

[SPEAKER A]: Yes please. I feel much clearer about it.

[SPEAKER B]: You did the right thing even raising it. Noticing and speaking up — that's exactly what keeps a child safe.

---
KEY TAKEAWAYS (for show notes / text card pairing):
1. Children's safeguarding applies anywhere a child might be — a reasonable concern is enough, you don't need evidence.
2. Report to your designated safeguarding lead straight away; call 999 first if a child is in immediate danger.
3. Know who your safeguarding lead is — and the NSPCC helpline (0808 800 5000) is there if internal routes aren't available.
