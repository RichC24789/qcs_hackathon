TOPIC: End of life care
DURATION TARGET: 5 minutes
ELEVENLABS VOICE SUGGESTION: Speaker A — gentle female (e.g. "Aria"), Speaker B — warm, compassionate male (e.g. "Marcus")

[SPEAKER A]: I'm not sure how to say this. One of the residents I've cared for for two years is now at the end of her life. The nurse mentioned a DNAR is in place, and honestly it threw me. Does that mean we stop caring for her? And I keep welling up — I don't know if I'm even allowed to feel like this about a resident.

[SPEAKER B]: Take a breath. First — yes, you're allowed to feel this. Caring for someone for two years and then walking with them to the end, of course it moves you. That's not unprofessional, that's human. We'll come back to looking after you, because that matters too.

[SPEAKER A]: Thank you. But the DNAR — it sounds so final. Like we've given up.

[SPEAKER B]: I understand why it feels that way, but it's really important you know this: a DNAR does not mean "do nothing." It's a specific medical decision, made by a doctor, that if her heart stops we won't attempt resuscitation — because it wouldn't help her and could cause distress. Everything else carries on. Comfort, dignity, pain relief, company, gentle conversation — all of that continues, and it matters more than ever now.

[SPEAKER A]: So I keep doing what I've always done, just... softer.

[SPEAKER B]: Exactly. That's palliative care — the focus shifts from cure to comfort and quality of life. Keeping her mouth comfortable, her position eased, her favourite music on, her family welcomed. Small things become the whole thing.

[SPEAKER A]: Is there anything I should be especially careful not to do?

[SPEAKER B]: Mainly, don't let the DNAR change how present you are with her. The biggest mistake is treating it as a reason to step back. If anything, lean in — your warmth in these days is part of her care.

[SPEAKER A]: She has one of those advance care plans, doesn't she? I've seen it mentioned but never really read it.

[SPEAKER B]: Please do read it. That plan records her wishes — how she wanted things to be, who she wanted near her. People sometimes avoid it because it feels too hard or too sad. But following it is how we honour what she actually wanted, instead of guessing.

[SPEAKER A]: What about her family? I never know what to say to them.

[SPEAKER B]: You don't need perfect words. Being present, being gentle, saying "we're keeping her comfortable" — that's enough. On who tells them what, and timing, I'll lead on that, so you're never carrying that responsibility alone.

[SPEAKER A]: And if she passes away when I'm on shift? I think that's what I'm frightened of.

[SPEAKER B]: That's a very normal fear. If it happens, we follow our procedure calmly. We don't move her. We contact the manager — me — and the GP confirms things. And we treat that moment with all the dignity she deserves. You won't be figuring it out by yourself; you call me and we do it together.

[SPEAKER A]: Okay. That actually helps to hear out loud.

[SPEAKER B]: And here's the part people skip: afterwards, you look after you. End of life care is emotionally demanding, and it's demanding because you care. Come and talk to me, lean on the team, don't bottle it up. Supporting each other after a death is part of the job, not a weakness.

[SPEAKER A]: I think I needed permission to find it hard.

[SPEAKER B]: Consider it given. The fact that it touches you is exactly why you're good at this. Let's go and read her care plan together, and then I'll check in with you again later.

[SPEAKER A]: Thank you. Really.

---
KEY TAKEAWAYS (for show notes / text card pairing):
1. A DNAR is a doctor's decision about resuscitation only — all other care, comfort, and dignity continue.
2. Read and follow the advance care plan; if someone dies on shift, follow your procedure and contact your manager — you won't do it alone.
3. Look after yourself and each other — end of life care is emotionally demanding, and support is part of the job.
