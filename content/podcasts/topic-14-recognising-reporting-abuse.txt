TOPIC: Recognising and reporting abuse
DURATION TARGET: 4–5 minutes
ELEVENLABS VOICE SUGGESTION: Speaker A — warm female (e.g. "Aria"), Speaker B — calm, reassuring male (e.g. "Marcus")

[SPEAKER A]: Can I run something past you? I've been worried about one of the gentlemen I support — I'll call him Mr Ellis. Yesterday I noticed bruising on his upper arm, and when I mentioned one of the other carers by name, he went really quiet and anxious. I've got no proof anything's wrong. I feel like I might be making something out of nothing.

[SPEAKER B]: First of all, I'm glad you came to me. And I want to say this clearly: you do not need proof. You don't even need to be sure. A concern is enough — that's the whole point of safeguarding. Your job isn't to work out what happened. It's to notice, and to tell someone.

[SPEAKER A]: That's the bit I find hard. It feels like I'm accusing a colleague.

[SPEAKER B]: I understand. But reporting isn't accusing. You're not deciding anyone is guilty — you're flagging something that didn't sit right, so the people whose job it is can look into it. If you stay quiet because you're not certain, that's when people get missed.

[SPEAKER A]: Okay. So what do I actually do? He hasn't said anything directly.

[SPEAKER B]: Two things. Write down exactly what you saw and what he did — factually. "Bruising on the upper right arm, became quiet and anxious when I mentioned a colleague's name." No guessing about how it happened. Then report it to me, or to the safeguarding lead, straight away.

[SPEAKER A]: And if he does open up to me — if he tells me something?

[SPEAKER B]: Then listen. Let him talk. Don't fire questions at him and don't put words in his mouth — no leading questions. And this one's really important: never promise to keep it secret. If he asks you to, gently explain that you may have to share it to keep him safe.

[SPEAKER A]: That's the part I'd struggle with. You want to reassure them.

[SPEAKER B]: You still can. You can say, "Thank you for telling me, you've done the right thing, and I'll make sure the right people help you." That's honest and kind at the same time.

[SPEAKER A]: What if the concern was about you — or about a manager?

[SPEAKER B]: Same duty, different route. If the person you'd normally report to is part of the concern, you go straight to the local authority safeguarding adults team. You're never stuck. There's always a way to raise it.

[SPEAKER A]: And the types of abuse — I always picture physical, but...

[SPEAKER B]: It's much broader. Physical, yes, but also emotional, sexual, financial, neglect, discrimination, and more. The signs can be physical, like unexplained injuries, or behavioural — withdrawal, anxiety, fear around a particular person. What you spotted with Mr Ellis is exactly that kind of behavioural sign. You were right to notice it.

[SPEAKER A]: Should I try to find out a bit more first, so I'm giving you something solid?

[SPEAKER B]: No — and I know that instinct comes from a good place. Investigating it yourself can actually get in the way of a proper enquiry, and it isn't yours to carry. Hand me what you've genuinely seen and heard, and let the safeguarding process take over from there.

[SPEAKER A]: So I'm not overreacting.

[SPEAKER B]: Not at all. You're doing your job well. Tell me the details now, I'll record it with you, and we'll get it reported today.

[SPEAKER A]: Thank you. I feel better having said it out loud.

[SPEAKER B]: That's what I'm here for. Always bring me the thing that doesn't feel right.

---
KEY TAKEAWAYS (for show notes / text card pairing):
1. A concern is enough — you don't need proof, and reporting is not the same as accusing.
2. Listen, never promise confidentiality, avoid leading questions, and write down what you saw factually.
3. Report to your manager or safeguarding lead straight away — and go to the local authority safeguarding team if a manager is involved.
