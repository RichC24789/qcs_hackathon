TOPIC: Consent to care
DURATION TARGET: 5 minutes
ELEVENLABS VOICE SUGGESTION: Speaker A — thoughtful female (e.g. "Aria"), Speaker B — warm, experienced male (e.g. "Marcus")

[SPEAKER A]: I could use some advice. There's a gentleman I support — he has dementia — and he's started refusing his shower, every time. I know he's meant to be kept clean and comfortable, but he says no, sometimes quite firmly. Part of me thinks I should just gently get on with it for his own good. Is that wrong?

[SPEAKER B]: I'm glad you asked rather than pushing ahead. The short answer is yes, you should pause. Consent isn't a signature he gave when he moved in — it's something you check before every single care task. If he's saying no, that "no" counts.

[SPEAKER A]: Even though it might not be good for him to skip washing?

[SPEAKER B]: Even then. People have the right to refuse care, even when refusing carries some risk. What we don't do is override him because we've decided what's best. What we do is respect the refusal in the moment, record it, and try to understand it.

[SPEAKER A]: But he has dementia. Can he really consent, or refuse?

[SPEAKER B]: That's the key question, and it's not all-or-nothing. Capacity is decision-specific. He may still show you clearly that he doesn't want this right now — and distress is a form of communication. Look for non-verbal signs: is he relaxed and going along with it, or tense, pulling away, upset? If he can't make the decision himself, then a best interest decision needs to be in place before that care goes ahead — not something you decide alone at the bathroom door.

[SPEAKER A]: So what do I do in the actual moment when he refuses?

[SPEAKER B]: Don't force it. Stop, stay calm, and note the refusal. Then come back to it later — often the answer is about timing or how it's offered. Is he cold? Frightened of the water? Would a wash feel less exposing than a shower? Would a different time of day, or a familiar carer, change things?

[SPEAKER A]: I hadn't thought about why. I was just seeing it as him being "difficult."

[SPEAKER B]: That reframe is everything. The refusal is information. And please, record it every time — don't skip it because it feels like you've failed. You haven't. A documented refusal protects him and you, and it builds the picture the team needs.

[SPEAKER A]: With another lady I support, she always says yes — so I suppose I can take that as read?

[SPEAKER B]: That's the very thing to avoid. Even when someone usually agrees, you still check each time — a person who's happy on Monday might not be on Thursday. Consent isn't a one-time tick; you ask fresh, every interaction.

[SPEAKER A]: Here's the tricky one. His daughter says, "Just go ahead, he won't mind once it's done." Do I listen to her?

[SPEAKER B]: Gently, no — not on its own. The service user's wishes take precedence. A family member can't consent on his behalf unless there's a proper best interest decision in place. So you'd thank her, explain that you have to respect his wishes in the moment, and bring it back to me so we follow the right process.

[SPEAKER A]: That makes sense. So: don't force it, record it, look for the reason, and check the best interest side with you.

[SPEAKER B]: Exactly. And tell me whenever consent is refused or you're unsure about his capacity — that's never a bother, that's the job done well.

[SPEAKER A]: Thank you. I feel better about letting him say no.

[SPEAKER B]: That's a lovely way to put it. Letting him keep his voice. That's good care.

---
KEY TAKEAWAYS (for show notes / text card pairing):
1. Consent is an ongoing conversation before every task — verbal consent is valid, and people can refuse even risky-to-refuse care.
2. With dementia, watch non-verbal signs; if someone lacks capacity, a best interest decision must be in place before care.
3. Record every refusal and report it — a family member can't override the person's wishes without a valid best interest decision.
