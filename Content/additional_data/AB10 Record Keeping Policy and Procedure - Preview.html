4. Policy

4.1

What is Recorded

{{{Client_Name_Official}}} recognises that it is vital that up-to-date, clear and comprehensive records are maintained.

Accurate record keeping is particularly important and essential to demonstrate best practice in the following areas:

  • To evidence that the appropriate {{{Support_or_care}}} has been delivered in a person centred way
  • To evidence that the relevant and appropriate documentation has been completed prior to the start of the service and then reviewed regularly:
    • Pre-service Assessment
    • Risk Assessments
    • {{{Care_plan_name}}}
  • Records of contact with family and others important to the {{{Service_user_text}}} are made to show that information has been shared with the correct individuals in a timely manner
  • Medication records are updated and reviewed regularly, especially at times of rapid change
  • Records will be generated and kept of all activities which may affect the quality of {{{Support_or_care}}} given, the continuity of that {{{Support_or_care}}}
  • To provide evidence that appropriate audits and other methods of monitoring have been undertaken
  • To provide detail of the handovers and exchanges of important information have taken place
  • Minutes of all team meetings, supervisions and training are recorded
  • To ensure that the latest information, guidance and best practice has been shared with staff to support the health and welfare of {{{Service_user_text}}}s
  • Records of any remote consultations or multidisciplinary inputs are maintained
  • Evidence of continuous learning and improvement
  • To show the {{{reg_authority}}} and {{{Local_Authority_Name}}} that {{{Client_Name_Official}}} has effectively supported {{{Service_user_text}}}s
  • Evidence of tackling business matters which affect the integrity of {{{Client_Name_Official}}} and the safety of {{{Service_user_text}}}s
4.2

Record Keeping for {{{Service_user_text}}}'s {{{Support_or_care}}} Provision

Record keeping is an integral part of the provision of care and support provided to {{{Service_user_text}}}s on a day-to-day basis and is the way that {{{Client_Name_Official}}} staff demonstrate:

  • What {{{Support_or_care}}} has been delivered
  • What {{{Support_or_care}}} has not been delivered and why (where applicable)
  • When the {{{Support_or_care}}} has been delivered
  • That the {{{Support_or_care}}} has been delivered safely, and in line with the correct {{{Care_plan_name}}} and risk assessments
  • That the {{{Support_or_care}}} is person-centred and relevant
  • That information that needs to be shared has been shared with the relevant people
4.3

Staff Who Supervise Others

All staff who supervise others are responsible for the content and quality of the notes written whilst under their supervision.

Examples of this could be:

  • Staff Induction
  • Staff Shadowing
  • Staff Training
  • Staff Competencies
  • Staff Supervisions
  • Staff Appraisals

Staff will ensure that they fully understand and follow their code of conduct in relation to record keeping.
4.4

Staff Responsibilities

All staff who make entries in records are responsible for the quality and content as well as adherence to this policy. Staff understand they are responsible and accountable for meeting the requirements laid down by:

  • Policies and procedures of {{{Client_Name_Official}}}
  • Their contract of employment
  • The Nursing and Midwifery Council (NMC) where applicable
  • The {{{reg_authority_name_long}}}

When developing any records, particularly {{{Care_plan_name}}}s, protocols and clinical records (where appropriate), it is the responsibility of {{{Reg_Manager_Name}}} to ensure that staff are adequately supervised and competent to undertake the delegated tasks within these plans/protocols and to meet any training needs identified to support effective implementation of these plans/protocols.
4.5

Staff Compliance

All staff must ensure that they comply with this policy and must report any related incidents involving breaches of confidentiality (including data loss) using the risk management procedures at {{{Client_Name_Official}}}.
4.6

Record Retention

{{{Client_Name_Official}}} will comply with record retention requirements and can refer to its Archiving, Disposal and Storing of Records Policy and Procedure.
4.7

Security

All records are stored securely and safely, with access given to authorised persons only.

5. Procedure

5.1

Acknowledging the Importance of Record Keeping

All staff must be aware of the following principles when completing any records at {{{Client_Name_Official}}}.

Records:

  • Provide a permanent legal record
  • Are a part of professional duty
  • Can be used as evidence in court, as well as for audit, investigative and complaints purposes
  • Promote good working practices, such as continuity of care and liaising with other agencies
  • Are essential to promote workplace accountability
5.2

Record Keeping - {{{Service_user_text}}} {{{Support_or_care}}}

{{{Client_Name_Official}}} will keep individual records (either paper-based or digital {{{Support_or_care}}} records) on the {{{Support_or_care}}} delivered with each {{{Service_user_text}}}. {{{Client_Name_Official}}} ensures that:

  • All {{{Support_or_care}}} records are accurate, honest and comprehensive
  • All {{{Support_or_care}}} records are updated at the time of service delivery and (in an emergency situation) as soon as possible afterwards
  • All entries that log in and out of the {{{Service_user_text}}}'s property digitally for {{{Support_or_care}}} are at the correct location where the {{{Support_or_care}}} is due to be provided 
  • All staff understand the method for record keeping
  • All staff understand the importance of reading the previous entry
  • Records will detail all activities relating to the provision of {{{Support_or_care}}} including but not limited to:
    • Personal care
    • Nutrition and hydration
    • Medication
    • Tissue viability
  • Times and dates should always be checked to confirm the records are accurate
  • The record is person-centred and can be understood by the {{{Service_user_text}}} - Staff should refer to the Accessible Information Standard Policy and Procedure
  • The record is recorded in a factual and clear way, in plain English, no jargon or words that have dual meaning
5.3

Standards for Healthcare Record Keeping

Standards required in record keeping may vary from profession to profession. Some standards apply to all healthcare professionals. These standards are:

  • Entries must be as objective as possible, and this means writing in a way that is exactly as the person has described. This ensures that entries are precise and accurate
  • Handwriting must be legible
  • The time and date that the entry is made must be included. This should be in real time and chronological order, and be as close to the actual time as possible
  • Each page must clearly identify the {{{Service_user_text}}} by recording their name and location
  • For paper-based entries, mistakes must be crossed through with a single line, signed, dated and timed. Correction fluid must not be used. Any sheets containing errors must not be removed from the records
  • Documentation must be recorded and stored, and must be accessible in chronological order
  • Entries must be made in black ink where handwritten records are made
  • All entries to records should be signed. In the case of written records, the person’s name and job title should be printed alongside the first entry
  • Records should be factual and not include unnecessary abbreviations, jargon, meaningless phrases or assumptions but if used, must only be those in widespread use within the profession
5.4

Guidelines for +


Record Keeping

  • Entries made must be objective, accurate and factual
  • Assumptions about people are avoided
  • Observation, as well as talking and listening effectively, will enable staff to identify {{{Service_user_text}}}s' needs
  • The completion of documentation must never be left until the end of the working day. Records must be completed as soon after the event as possible to avoid forgetting valuable information
  • Records must be contemporaneous
  • Events may happen which will need to be reported immediately to a senior member of staff, such as accidents, incidents, serious hazards and complaints. If there is no immediate way of recording such matters, the member of staff must still make their own records
  • {{{Service_user_text}}}s must be made aware of any information kept about them and play an active part in their own {{{Support_or_care}}} planning and communications. Records should evidence their awareness and involvement
  • Ensure there are records and details of any assessments and reviews undertaken and provide clear evidence of the arrangements made for future and ongoing {{{Support_or_care}}}. This should also include details of information given about care and treatment
  • Records should identify any risks or problems that have arisen and show the action taken to deal with them
  • Records must not be altered or destroyed without authorisation to do so
  • Records should be readable when photocopied or scanned
  • Records must never be falsified
  • Significant events and actions documented are:
    • Evident
    • Easily located
    • Legible
    • Easily understood
    • Relevant and truthful
    • Signed by the entrant
5.5

Types of {{{Service_user_text}}} Records of {{{Support_or_care}}}

The types of records that are kept relating to the {{{Service_user_text}}} and their {{{Support_or_care}}} include but are not limited to the following documents:

  • {{{Service_user_text}}} {{{Care_plan_name}}}s
  • {{{Service_user_text}}} risk assessments
  • Daily {{{Support_or_care}}} record/log
  • {{{MAR_Chart}}}
  • Body maps
  • Fluid charts
  • Financial transaction record
5.6

Formats of Record Keeping

{{{Client_Name_Official}}} recognises that there is no one size fits all method for record keeping and may use a paper-based and/or digital based record system. Many methods may be used for keeping records within the organisation. {{{Client_Name_Official}}} understands the importance of having clear, concise methods for recording and logging information as well as the importance of staff understanding which method to implement at which time.
5.7

Accessibility of Digital Records

{{{Client_Name_Official}}} recognises that {{{Service_user_text}}} records are confidential, and access is only given to relevant individuals. The {{{Service_user_text}}} reserves the right to access their records at any time and must give consent for their records to be shared with other parties. Where {{{Client_Name_Official}}} operates via a digital record system, it will ensure that {{{Service_user_text}}}s have the opportunity to access their records as they wish.

{{{Client_Name_Official}}} has a secure method of operating the digital {{{Support_or_care}}} record system which is password protected, offering access to authorised individuals only.

{{{Client_Name_Official}}} understands the importance of operating a read-only system for digital record sharing, for times when information should be shared without the risk of it being amended.
5.8

Office-based Computer Held Records

  • Personal information held on computers must be password protected to avoid the risk of breaching confidentiality
  • There must be access controls in place to restrict users of the system to specific functions as defined by the system manager
  • Screens must not be left unattended when the system is active
  • Steps must be taken to make regular backups of computer held records
  • Backups must be stored in a secure place, if possible, in a separate location
5.9

Guidelines for Dealing with Messages

  • Write down messages clearly and legibly
  • Listen carefully and check for accuracy with the person transmitting the message
  • Work out a scale of urgency for transmitting messages
  • If a message is left with someone else to pass on, be sure that the person it is intended for actually gets it

When writing down messages, include the following:

  • Name of the person sending the message
  • Name of the person who is to receive the message, date and time the message has been received and given, clear details of the communicated message and an indication of the urgency
  • Whether it was a verbal message or telephone message

Where appropriate, the message should be evidenced on the relevant {{{Service_user_text}}}'s record.
5.10

Confidentiality

All staff need to be aware of the legal requirements for maintaining confidentiality and ensure that their practice, including record keeping practice, is in accordance with these requirements regarding maintaining and sharing records.

All staff need to ensure that they do not discuss {{{Service_user_text}}}s in places where they may be overheard or leave records in an unsecure area where they may be seen by unauthorised persons. This includes electronic records on computer screens.

Information that can identify the {{{Service_user_text}}} must not be used or disclosed for purposes other than healthcare without the individual’s explicit consent unless, in exceptional circumstances, this is required legally or where there is a wider public interest.

Staff must also refer to Mental Capacity Act (MCA) 2005 Policy and Procedure at {{{Client_Name_Official}}}.
5.11

Data Protection

Records kept within {{{Client_Name_Official}}} must be reviewed, retained and destroyed in accordance with recommended retention and disposal schedules.

Staff can refer to the Archiving, Disposal and Storing of Records Policy and Procedure at {{{Client_Name_Official}}} for further information.

The collection of any information that is recorded will follow the principles set out in the UK General Data Protection Regulations, underpinned by the overarching need to gain consent from the {{{Service_user_text}}} and through open and transparent discussions about how personal information is used within {{{Client_Name_Official}}}.

Staff can refer to the following policies for further information:

  • Consent to Care and Treatment Policy and Procedure
  • Mental Capacity Act (MCA) 2005 Policy and Procedure
  • Data Protection and Confidentiality Policy and Procedure
  • Access to Information Policy and Procedure
  • UK GDPR suite of policies and procedures

Staff should also access further guidance provided from the ICO relating directly to the management of healthcare data.
5.12

Safe Secure Storage of Personal Information

Within {{{Client_Name_Official}}} there must be a secure storage area provided to maintain confidentiality. Within the {{{Service_user_text}}}'s personal environment, or during periods of social leave, it is recommended that a secure storage area is provided to maintain confidentiality.

This is subject to the agreement of the {{{Service_user_text}}} and/or representative. If storage is refused or there is a refusal to retain information within their personal environment then this should be in written form and kept with the main {{{Care_plan_name}}}.

The location and access to this storage must be by agreement with other health and emergency professionals to ensure all needs and communication routes are met.

Forms may be needed in an emergency when {{{Support_or_care}}} staff are not available to give access, such as:

  • DNA CPR forms
  • MARs
  • Advance Care Plans

Examples of secure areas can be a lockable drawer or cupboards, as these can be part of the home fixtures and fittings. There are other alternatives to these such as a lockable filing wallet or unit. Ideally, an information label should be on the front of any storage area stating 'confidential' alongside information on how health professionals can gain access.

In many cases, this is done by a key safe.
5.13

Files Away from {{{Client_Name_Official}}}

Where personal information for any {{{Service_user_text}}} needs to be used outside {{{Client_Name_Official}}}, it must be stored securely.

Any files and information that need to be transported with a member of staff who is authorised to have access to the information, for example, if they are needed for providing {{{Support_or_care}}}, auditing or a professionals' meeting then they must be transported in a secure way such as lockable case, bag or a lockable filing unit in the boot of a car (out of public sight). Any such documents must not be left in vehicles overnight.

Where records are routinely transferred to or away from {{{Client_Name_Official}}} this must be reflected within the {{{Care_plan_name}}} and Risk Assessment process.
5.14

Training and Education

All staff will receive a thorough induction into record keeping requirements, the security of records, the confidentiality principles and data protection on commencement at {{{Client_Name_Official}}} and via individual support on an as-and-when needed basis.

Staff will be made aware of the following:-

  • The need to maintain accurate records
  • Ensuring that the information recorded is factual and can be understood correctly
  • The importance of logging the correct time and date on the entry
  • The importance of being honest in the account and the life-changing risks associated with dishonesty in record keeping
  • Confidentiality
  • UK GDPR

{{{Client_Name_Official}}} will identify and support the training needs of all staff who may have specific requirements in relation to {{{Service_user_text}}} records, such as person-centred planning.
5.15

The above methods can be applied to the following types of record keeping:

  • {{{Service_user_text}}} {{{Support_or_care}}}/clinical records
  • Staff communications
  • Memos
  • Diary and handover systems between staff
  • Meeting minutes
  • Audits and report writing
  • Supervisions or appraisals
  • Letters, e-mail or fax communication

6. Definitions

6.1 Records
  • Records are defined as ‘recorded information, in any form, created or received and maintained by an organisation in the transaction of its business or conduct of affairs and kept as evidence of such activity’
6.2 Health Record
  • A 'health record' means any record which consists of information relating to the physical or mental health or condition of an individual and has been made by or on behalf of a health professional in connection with the care of that individual
6.3 Handover
  • This is the transfer of responsibility and accountability for some or all aspects of care to another person on a temporary or permanent basis
6.4 UK GDPR
  • The General Data Protection Regulation 2016/679 is a regulation in EU law on data protection and privacy for all individuals within the European Union and the European Economic Area. It was implemented on 25th May 2018
  • Following the UK leaving the EU, the General Data Protection Regulation has been retained in UK law and will continue to be read alongside the Data Protection Act 2018. Some technical amendments will take place to ensure that it can function in UK law
6.5 Abbreviations
  • A shortened form of a word or phrase
6.6 Jargon
  • Special words or expressions used by a particular profession or group that may be difficult to understand
6.7 Assumption
  • A thing or event that is believed to be true or certain to happen without proof

7. Key Facts - Professionals

  • Information is of greatest value when it is accurate, up-to-date and accessible when needed
  • All staff should be trained to implement and operate a good and robust standard of record keeping
  • {{{Client_Name_Official}}} is dependent on its records to operate efficiently and account for its actions or inactions
  • Records are a legal requirement
  • Staff must be able to prioritise record keeping in their daily role to support effective communication. For effective auditing, 'if it's not written down, it's not done'

8. Key Facts - People Affected by The Service

  • {{{Care_plan_name}}}s and health records will contain information about you and the support you need. This is important so that every member of staff involved with your care has access to what matters most to you
  • There are strict laws and regulations to ensure that your health records are kept confidential and can only be accessed by people directly involved in your care
  • You are encouraged to be fully involved in every aspect of your care and how this is delivered. Your health records will be used to record this information
  • It is your right to review your records on request. If you wish, you can also request for someone else to view them. Staff will be able to advise you on request

  • Feedback from sources such as external visiting professionals, {{{Service_user_text}}}s, staff and families is positive in relation to communication systems and record keeping
  • The wide understanding of the policy is enabled by proactive use of the QCS App
  • Staff receive robust training in completing records in various formats in line with the Accessible Information Standard, that support the {{{Service_user_text}}}'s communication and understanding needs.
  • There is evidence that records are person-centered, completed at the right time and in line with the principles outlined in this policy.
  • Themed audits take place to ensure that systems and processes are in place, which are efficient and regularly reviewed, with regard to record keeping and communication with staff and {{{Service_user_text}}}s
  • Where issues have arisen with regard to communication and record keeping, there is a 'no blame' culture in {{{Client_Name_Official}}}, but opportunity is taken to reflect on practice, review and implement changes for better outcomes
