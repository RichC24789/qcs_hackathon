5. Procedure

5.1

Chain of Infection

The Chain of Infection explains how infections spread. It has six links:

  • Pathogen – The germ that causes disease (e.g. norovirus, MRSA, influenza)
  • Reservoir – Where the pathogen lives and multiplies (people, environment, equipment, food or drink)
  • Portal of exit – How the pathogen leaves the body (faeces, urine, blood, vomit, sneezing)
  • Mode of transmission – How the pathogen spreads (hands, equipment, droplets, airborne)
  • Portal of entry – How the pathogen enters the body (inhalation, eyes, nose, mouth, wounds)
  • Susceptible host – A person at risk due to age, low immunity, or health conditions

Breaking the Chain of Infection

Infection can be prevented by breaking the chain at any point:

  • Pathogen – Complete prescribed antibiotics to reduce resistance
  • Reservoir – Clean and disinfect; isolate or distance infectious individuals
  • Portal of exit – Cover mouth and nose when sneezing or coughing
  • Transmission – Maintain good hand hygiene
  • Portal of entry – Cover wounds and use PPE such as face masks
  • Susceptible host – Vaccination to improve immunity
5.2

Respiratory Hygiene and Cough Etiquette

Respiratory hygiene and cough etiquette must be followed by staff at {{{Client_Name_Official}}} as part of standard infection prevention and control measures. To reduce the spread of respiratory infections:

  • Cover the nose and mouth with a disposable, single-use tissue when coughing, sneezing, wiping, or blowing the nose
  • Dispose of used tissues immediately into a waste bin
  • Clean hands with soap and water after coughing, sneezing, using tissues, or contact with respiratory secretions or contaminated surfaces
  • Avoid touching the eyes, nose, and mouth with unclean hands
  • Wear appropriate PPE when providing care to a coughing {{{Service_user_text}}}
  • Recommend symptomatic {{{Service_user_text}}}s wear a fluid-resistant surgical face mask in communal areas to reduce droplet spread
  • Clean and disinfect frequently touched surfaces regularly
5.3

Cleaning

Environmental cleaning and disinfection is carried out in line with the Health and Social Care Act 2008: Code of Practice on the Prevention and Control of Infections and the {{{reg_authority}}} Regulation 12: Safe Care and Treatment, ensuring that care is provided in a safe environment for {{{Service_user_text}}}s, staff and visitors. Cleaning practices are informed by risk assessment and the needs of {{{Client_Name_Official}}}.

Staff must:

  • Wear appropriate PPE (gloves and plastic apron), with eye protection where there is a risk of splashing
  • Prepare fresh cleaning solutions for each task, diluted correctly according to manufacturers' instructions, and not mix products unless confirmed as safe
  • Clean using warm water and general-purpose detergent with disposable cloths or paper towels, changing solutions regularly to prevent contamination
  • Allow surfaces to air dry where possible and dispose of used solutions promptly in a designated sluice or dirty utility area
  • Remove PPE and perform hand hygiene immediately after cleaning, before undertaking other duties

Use of Disinfectants

Disinfectants are prepared and used only by trained staff, in accordance with their training and risk assessments, to ensure safe and effective use.

Disinfectants are used in the following situations, supporting safe care and outbreak control:

  • Cleaning food preparation areas contaminated with blood or faeces
  • Decontamination of isolation rooms
  • During outbreaks, or when directed by the Infection Prevention and Control or Health Protection Team

All disinfectants are clearly labelled, stored securely, and managed in accordance with COSHH regulations and substance-specific risk assessments, demonstrating compliance with legal health and safety requirements. Gloves and plastic aprons are worn when handling disinfectants; eye protection is available where splashing is possible.

5.4

Hand Hygiene

Most infections can be prevented through good hand hygiene, performed at the right times and in the correct way. Routine hand washing removes dirt and transient microorganisms from the hands. All staff at {{{Client_Name_Official}}} must wash their hands:

  • Before and after {{{Service_user_text}}} contact, treatments or interventions
  • Before eating, preparing, or handling food
  • Before donning and after removing PPE
  • After using the toilet, sneezing, blowing the nose, cleaning, or any activity that contaminates the hands

Liquid Soap

Hand washing with liquid soap and water removes dirt and organic material and must be carried out:

  • Before and after direct contact with {{{Service_user_text}}}s, and after contact with body fluids, even if gloves have been worn
  • When hands are visibly dirty or soiled, or after contact with {{{Service_user_text}}}s with diarrhoea, vomiting, clostridium difficile, or norovirus
  • After multiple consecutive applications of alcohol gel or rub

Alcohol Hand-Rub

  • Alcohol-based hand rubs (60–80% alcohol) are effective on visibly clean hands and are recommended by the World Health Organisation (WHO)
  • They are not suitable for visibly soiled hands or against organisms such as C. difficile and norovirus, where handwashing with soap and water is required
  • Hand rubs are quick to use, skin-friendly, suitable for point-of-care use, and acceptable for Muslim use as they contain synthetic alcohol
  • Hands must be clean; wash with soap and water first if visibly soiled
  • Apply one shot and avoid excess to protect the skin
  • Rub hands thoroughly for 10-15 seconds or until the gel evaporates, covering all surfaces: palms, backs, fingers, thumbs, webs, and wrists

Hand Washing Technique Using Liquid Soap

  • Expose wrists and forearms, wet hands under running warm water, and apply the recommended amount of liquid soap
  • Rub all parts of the hands vigorously for 40–60 seconds using the six-step technique:
  • Palm to palm, then backs of hands over opposite palms
  • Palm to palm with fingers interlaced, then backs of fingers on opposing palms
  • Around each thumb, then palms with opposing fingers
  • Rinse thoroughly under running water
  • Tip: Washing for the duration of singing 'Happy Birthday' twice helps ensure adequate time

Hand Drying and Skin Care

  • Dry hands thoroughly after washing. Wet hands transfer germs more easily and increase skin damage
  • Use disposable paper towels where possible; pat hands dry instead of rubbing
  • Avoid putting on gloves while hands are still wet
  • Use individual emollient hand cream regularly. Avoid communal pots
  • Monitor for skin irritation. If it occurs, review technique and inform {{{Infection_Prevention_Control_Lead_Name}}}
  • Be aware that damaged skin increases germ growth and infection risk

Bare Below the Elbows and Cultural Considerations

To prevent the spread of infection, staff at {{{Client_Name_Official}}} must follow these hygiene standards:

  • Hands and arms up to the elbows/mid-forearm must be exposed and free from clothing/jewellery during direct care
  • Nails must be short, clean, with no nail polish or extensions
  • Wristwatches and bracelets should not be worn; alert bracelets may be attached to a lanyard or pinned to the uniform
  • Rings: Only one plain band; rings with stones are not permitted

Cultural and Religious Considerations:

  • Uniforms may include full or three-quarter length sleeves when not providing direct care
  • Sleeves must not be loose or dangling and should be rolled or secured during handwashing and care activities
  • Sikh staff wearing a Kara bracelet should ensure it is pushed up the arm and secured during handwashing and direct care
5.5

Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

Staff must wear PPE provided by {{{Client_Name_Official}}} whenever there is a risk of exposure to infection, blood, or body fluids. All staff are trained in the correct selection, use and disposal of PPE, including:

  • Proper use - approved procedures for donning, removing, and disposing
  • When to use each type
  • When to replace
  • Understanding the limitations of the PPE in use
  • Reporting issues regarding the quality, quantity, or effectiveness of PPE to {{{Infection_Prevention_Control_Lead_Name}}}

All PPE provided for use by {{{Client_Name_Official}}} complies with the relevant BS/EN standards (European technical standards as adopted in the UK).

Gloves

Gloves are worn when exposure to blood, body fluids, non-intact skin, or mucous membranes is likely, to protect hands and prevent cross-infection. The following procedure is followed when donning gloves:

  • Before putting on gloves, hand hygiene is performed. Gloves are checked to make sure they are appropriate for the task, well-fitting, comfortable and suitable for the substances and duration of contact
  • Gloves are changed immediately after each {{{Service_user_text}}}, after completing a procedure or task, or if a perforation or puncture is suspected
  • After removing gloves, hand hygiene is performed to ensure any contamination is removed
  • Disposable, single-use, powder-free, low-protein, latex-free gloves are used for contact with body substances, contaminated items, mucous membranes, or non-intact skin

Masks

  • Surgical face masks are worn to protect the staff member’s mouth and nose during procedures that may produce splashes or aerosols of body substances
  • Particulate filter masks or other respiratory protective equipment is used when there is a clinical indication, such as procedures generating airborne particles or exposure to infectious agents
  • Masks must fit properly, cover both the nose and mouth, and be used according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Single-use masks are discarded after use, and hand hygiene performed before and after handling masks

Eye Protection/Face Visors

  • Goggles, visors, or protective glasses are worn to protect the team member’s eyes from exposure to splashes, aerosols or other contaminants from body substances or chemicals
  • Eye protection should fit securely and comfortably to ensure complete coverage of the eyes
  • Protective equipment is cleaned, disinfected or disposed of according to the manufacturer’s instructions and local infection control policies. Hand hygiene is performed before and after handling eye protection

Aprons

Single-use disposable plastic aprons are worn whenever there is a risk of exposure to body substances, chemicals, or cleaning agents:

  • Ensure the apron fully covers clothing or uniform to protect the wearer
  • Prevent contamination of the {{{Service_user_text}}} from the wearer’s clothing or uniform
  • The apron is used as a barrier against splashes during procedures or cleaning tasks
  • The apron is disposed of immediately after use and hand hygiene is performed

Faulty PPE

All PPE is inspected before use and replaced if damaged or inadequate:

  • Gloves and aprons are replaced if damaged, torn, or punctured
  • Face masks are replaced if damaged, soiled, damp, uncomfortable, or difficult to breathe through
  • Eye protection is replaced or decontaminated if damaged, soiled, or uncomfortable

Staff at {{{Client_Name_Official}}} report to {{{Infection_Prevention_Control_Lead_Name}}} any:

  • Concerns with the quality of PPE provided for the purposes of infection prevention and control
  • Concerns regarding stock levels, supply and range of PPE provided for the purposes of infection prevention and control
  • Issues raised by staff regarding the use of PPE provided for the purposes of infection prevention and control
  • Failure of staff to adhere to the PPE guidelines

Removal of PPE

PPE must be removed in this order:

  • Single-use gloves: Remove by holding the cuff and peeling off one glove, then peel the second glove over the first, enclosing it. Dispose of both gloves in the clinical waste bin and wash your hands
  • Apron: Unfasten or break apron ties at the neck and let the apron fold down on itself. Break ties at the waist and fold apron in on itself and discard
  • Protective eyewear: Remove with both hands, pull away from face and discard. Wash your hands
  • Mask: Untie or break the bottom ties first, then the top ties or elastic, and remove the mask by the ties. Lean forward slightly, discard in the clinical waste bin and wash your hands

{{{Infection_Prevention_Control_Lead_Name}}} is responsible for ensuring that PPE is available, that there are sufficient supplies and that it is properly maintained, cleaned and kept in good condition if it is reusable.

Uniform and Workwear

Management of Clinical Sharps

Where {{{Client_Name_Official}}} performs exposure prone procedures (EPPs), appropriate controls are in place to protect staff and {{{Service_user_text}}}s in accordance with the Health and Social Care Act 2008 Code of Practice and {{{reg_authority}}} Regulation 12: Safe Care and Treatment.

  • EPPs are recognised as procedures where injury to a staff member could expose a {{{Service_user_text}}}’s open tissues to the staff member’s blood, including contact with sharp instruments or needle tips
  • The increased risk of exposure to blood-borne Viruses (BBVs) associated with EPPs is identified and managed
  • Staff who know or suspect they may have a BBV must inform {{{Infection_Prevention_Control_Lead_Name}}} so appropriate assessment and advice can be provided; this does not automatically require a change of role
  • Staff with BBVs are assessed and, where necessary, restricted from EPPs that could place others at risk or affect their own health

Where Sharp Instruments are Used at {{{Client_Name_Official}}}:

  • Sharps are stored securely and kept out of reach of children
  • Clinical sharps are single-use only; needles are not re-sheathed, bent, or broken, and damaged sharps are disposed of immediately
  • Needles and syringes are disposed of as a single unit and are never dismantled by hand
  • Sharps are discarded immediately after use directly into an approved sharps container
  • Sharps containers are assembled correctly, not overfilled (no more than the manufacturer’s fill line or ¾ full), and the temporary closure mechanism is used between disposals
  • Once full, sharps containers are sealed and disposed of in accordance with manufacturer’s instructions and local policy
  • Items are never retrieved from sharps containers, and sharps are never pressed down to create more space
  • Sharps containers are labelled when first used and when sealed, carried by the handle, kept upright, and stored securely away from the floor, shoulder height, direct sunlight, or unsupervised access
  • If sharps are spilled, a safe retrieval method (e.g. dustpan and brush) is used and items are placed carefully into the container

Actions in the Event of a Sharps Injury

Immediate first aid must be performed on the exposed area as follows:

  • Gently encourage bleeding of the wound without scrubbing or sucking the area
  • Wash or irrigate the wound with soap and warm running water; do not use disinfectants or alcohol, and cover with a waterproof dressing
  • For exposures to eyes or mouth, rinse/irrigate thoroughly with copious amounts of water; if contact lenses are worn, remove before and after irrigation, and do not replace them immediately. Do not swallow the rinse water from the mouth
  • All sharps injuries must be reported immediately to the first aider and {{{Infection_Prevention_Control_Lead_Name}}}; if unavailable, seek treatment from an appropriate healthcare provider
  • {{{Client_Name_Official}}} will seek advice from occupational health services on the actions to take and how to obtain post-exposure prophylaxis in the event that occupational exposure to blood or body fluids is identified

For further information see the Needlestick Injury and Sharps Policy and Procedure.

Blood-borne Viruses (BBVs)

BBVs are viruses that some people carry in their blood which may cause severe disease in certain people, with few or no symptoms at all in others. These viruses can spread to another person whether the carrier of the virus is ill or not. The main BBVs of concern are:

  • Hepatitis B virus (HBV), Hepatitis C virus and Hepatitis D virus all of which cause hepatitis - a disease of the liver
  • Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV) which causes Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome (AIDS) affecting the immune system of the body. These viruses can also be found in body fluids other than blood, for example, semen, vaginal secretions and breast milk

Other body fluids and materials, including urine, faeces, saliva, sputum, sweat, tears, and vomit, carry a minimal risk of blood-borne virus (BBV) transmission unless they are visibly contaminated with blood. Both staff at {{{Client_Name_Official}}} and {{{Service_user_text}}}s face equal risk of BBV transmission from one another, highlighting the importance of consistent infection prevention and control measures.

All staff at {{{Client_Name_Official}}} who are at risk of exposure to BBVs are offered vaccination against Hepatitis B. Staff must ensure that all cuts and abrasions are covered with a waterproof dressing before providing care, and those with existing skin conditions are advised to seek guidance from their General Practitioner (GP) to reduce the risk of infection through open skin lesions.

Sepsis

Sepsis is a common and potentially life-threatening medical emergency that occurs when the body’s response to an infection becomes dysregulated. This immune overreaction can lead to tissue damage, organ failure and death if not treated quickly. Early identification and prompt treatment are therefore essential. In line with Regulation 12 of the Health and Social Care Act 2008 (Regulated Activities) Regulations 2014, which requires providers to deliver safe care and treatment, staff at {{{Client_Name_Official}}} must act immediately where sepsis is suspected.

Preventing infection is the most effective way of reducing the risk of sepsis. Robust infection prevention and control measures at {{{Client_Name_Official}}} are essential. These measures include:

  • Effective hand hygiene
  • Maintaining clean environments
  • Using appropriate PPE 
  • Ensuring the prompt and appropriate care of all wounds to prevent infection

Where an infection does occur, it must be recognised and treated without delay to prevent deterioration into sepsis. Staff at {{{Client_Name_Official}}} must be able to identify the early signs and symptoms of sepsis, such as:

  • Fever or abnormally low temperature
  • Rapid heart rate
  • Rapid breathing
  • Confusion
  • Reduced urine output
  • Any sudden deterioration

6. Definitions

6.1 Sharps Injury
  • A sharp includes items such as needles, sharp-edged instruments, broken glassware, any other item that may be contaminated with blood or body fluids and may cause laceration or puncture wounds, such as razors, sharp tissues, spicules of bone and teeth
  • Occupational exposure including needlestick (sharps) injury refers to the following injuries or exposures:
    • Percutaneous injury (from needles, instruments, bone fragments, human bites which break the skin)
    • Exposure of broken skin (abrasions, cuts, eczema)
    • Exposure of mucous membranes including the eyes, nose and/or mouth
6.2 Sepsis
  • Sepsis can lead to multiple organ failure and death especially if not recognised early and treated quickly
  • Sepsis is a life-threatening condition that arises when the body's response to an infection causes it to attack its own tissues and organs. In sepsis, the {{{Service_user_text}}}’s immune system goes into overdrive, setting off a series of reactions including widespread inflammation. This can cause a significant decrease in blood pressure, reducing the blood supply to vital organs and starving them of oxygen
6.3 Outbreak
  • An outbreak can be defined as two or more cases of infection occurring around the same time in {{{Service_user_text}}}s and/or their family members or carers, or an increase in the number of cases normally observed. The commonest outbreaks are due to viral respiratory infections and gastroenteritis. The organisms may be spread by hand contact and, on occasion, by other routes, which may include food
6.4 Communicable Diseases
  • Communicable diseases can be defined as illnesses caused by microorganisms and transmitted from an infected person or animal to another person or animal. Some diseases are passed on by direct or indirect contact with infected persons or with their excretions. Most diseases are spread through contact or close proximity because the causative bacteria or viruses are airborne, i.e. they can be expelled from the nose and mouth of the infected person and inhaled by anyone in the vicinity. Such diseases include diphtheria, scarlet fever, measles, mumps, whooping cough, influenza and smallpox. Some infectious diseases can be spread only indirectly, usually through contaminated food or water, for example, typhoid, cholera and dysentery. There are other infections that are introduced into the body by animal or insect carriers, for example, rabies, malaria and encephalitis
6.5 Pandemic
  • An epidemic occurring worldwide, or over a very wide area, crossing international boundaries and usually affecting a large number of people
6.6 Reporting of Injuries, Diseases and Dangerous Occurrences Regulations 2013 (RIDDOR)
  • RIDDOR requires employers and others to report deaths, certain types of injury, some occupational diseases and dangerous occurrences that ‘arise out of or in connection with work’
  • Generally, this covers incidents where the work activities, equipment or environment (including how work is carried out, organised or supervised) contributed in some way to the circumstances of the accident
6.7 Decontamination
  • Describes the combination of cleaning, disinfection and sterilisation to make re-useable items safe to use
6.8 Disinfection
  • A process that is used to kill or remove pathogenic microorganisms but which cannot kill bacterial spores
  • It can be used on both equipment and environmental surfaces
6.9 Sterilisation
  • A range of processes used to render the device free from microorganisms, including spores
6.10 High Consequence Infectious Diseases (HCID)
  • In the UK, a high consequence infectious disease (HCID) is defined according to the following criteria:
    • Acute infectious disease
    • Typically has a high case-fatality rate
    • May not have effective prophylaxis or treatment
    • Often difficult to recognise and detect rapidly
    • Ability to spread in the community and within healthcare settings
    • Requires an enhanced individual, population and system response to ensure it is managed effectively, efficiently and safely
  • HCIDs are further divided into contact and airborne groups:
    • Contact HCIDs are usually spread by direct contact with an infected patient or infected fluids, tissues and other materials, or by indirect contact with contaminated materials and fomites
    • Airborne HCIDs are spread by respiratory droplets or aerosol transmission, in addition to contact routes of transmission

  • Staff at {{{Client_Name_Official}}} have a duty of care to protect {{{Service_user_text}}}s from communicable diseases, including by keeping their own vaccinations up to date
  • Ensure the correct bins are used to dispose of waste, that the working areas are kept clean, that hands are washed afterwards and that all contaminated waste is safely disposed of
  • Ensure that all staff have up-to-date training on infection control
  • Ensure cleaning checklists are completed correctly for all treatment rooms/areas, signed and dated
  • Confirm the availability of the manufacturers' advice and instructions in respect of use, maintenance, decontamination and reuse of equipment and devices to avoid the risk of cross infection

8. Key Facts - People Affected by The Service

  • Personal protective equipment (PPE) will be worn by team members at {{{Client_Name_Official}}} when necessary to reduce the risk of spreading or coming into contact with germs, helping keep you safe
  • {{{Client_Name_Official}}} will ensure that staff who have a duty of care to themselves and {{{Service_user_text}}}s will be offered immunisation screening and vaccination to keep you safe
  • Washing your hands regularly is one of the simplest and most effective ways to prevent infections
