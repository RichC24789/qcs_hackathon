# Topic 2: Medication refusals — process, documentation, escalation

**Theme:** Medication
**Primary format:** Text card
**Secondary format:** Scenario quiz

---

## Hook

A service user has the right to refuse medication — your job is to respond safely, record it accurately, and know when to escalate.

---

## Key points

- Never force or coerce a service user to take medication — this could constitute abuse
- Calmly explore the reason for refusal — they may be confused, in pain, or have a valid concern
- Record the refusal clearly on the MAR chart, noting the reason given if one was provided
- Inform your manager, especially if the medication is high risk or time-critical
- Some refusals require a GP call — insulin, epilepsy medication, and blood thinners are examples

---

## Quick decision prompt

- **Service user refuses once but usually takes medication?** → Note it on the MAR chart, inform your manager, monitor for any change
- **Service user refuses repeatedly over several days?** → Escalate to manager and GP — a medication review may be needed
- **Service user lacks capacity and refuses?** → Do not administer without a best interest decision in place — contact your manager immediately

---

## Common mistakes

- Leaving the MAR chart blank rather than recording the refusal — a blank entry looks like a missed dose, not a refusal
- Trying to hide medication in food or drink without a formal covert medication plan in place
- Not informing the GP when a high-risk medication has been refused more than once

---

## Who to tell

- **Your manager** — same shift, sooner if medication is high risk
- **GP or prescriber** — if refusal is repeated or medication is clinically critical
- **Family or next of kin** — depending on the service user's wishes and capacity

---

## Quiz notes

Scenarios: service user with capacity refuses, service user without capacity refuses, staff member finds medication hidden under pillow. Ask what the correct response is in each case.
