# Topic 17: Whistleblowing — when to use it, protections, process

**Theme:** Safeguarding
**Primary format:** Micro podcast
**Secondary format:** Text card

---

## Hook

Raising a concern about unsafe or unethical practice is not disloyalty — it is one of the most important things you can do to protect the people you care for.

---

## Key points

- Whistleblowing means raising a concern about wrongdoing, risk, or malpractice in the workplace
- You are legally protected from dismissal or detriment if you raise a genuine concern in good faith
- You can raise concerns internally with your manager, or externally with CQC, the local authority, or other bodies
- You do not need proof — a reasonable belief that something is wrong is enough
- Anonymous reporting is possible but named reports are easier to investigate

---

## Quick decision prompt

- **Concerned about a colleague's practice but worried about the consequences of reporting?** → You are legally protected — raise it with your manager or safeguarding lead
- **Concerned about your manager's practice?** → Go above them — contact the registered manager, the safeguarding lead, or report directly to CQC
- **Concern has been raised internally but nothing has happened?** → Escalate externally — CQC, the local authority, or the care worker's professional body

---

## Common mistakes

- Not reporting because "it's probably nothing" — if something feels wrong, it is worth raising
- Raising a concern informally in conversation rather than making a documented report
- Believing that being anonymous means you will not be protected — named reporters have stronger legal protection

---

## Who to tell

- **Your manager or safeguarding lead** — first step in most cases
- **CQC** — can receive concerns directly at 03000 616161
- **Local authority safeguarding team** — for concerns about adults at risk
- **Protect (formerly Public Concern at Work)** — free whistleblowing advice at 020 3117 2520

---

## Podcast notes

Focus on the emotional reality of whistleblowing — the fear of consequences, loyalty to colleagues, uncertainty about whether it is serious enough. Reassure throughout. Cover legal protections plainly. 4–5 minutes.
