# Topic 18: Mental Capacity Act — principles, assessment, best interest decisions

**Theme:** Safeguarding
**Primary format:** Micro podcast
**Secondary format:** Scenario quiz

---

## Hook

The Mental Capacity Act protects people's right to make their own decisions — including decisions you might disagree with.

---

## Key points

- The five principles of the MCA: assume capacity unless proven otherwise; support decision-making before concluding lack of capacity; an unwise decision does not mean lack of capacity; act in best interests if capacity is lacking; use the least restrictive option
- Capacity is decision-specific and time-specific — someone may have capacity for some decisions but not others
- A capacity assessment must be carried out and documented before concluding someone lacks capacity
- Best interest decisions must involve the person as much as possible, and relevant family, carers, and professionals
- You cannot make a best interest decision alone — it requires consultation and documentation

---

## Quick decision prompt

- **Service user is making a decision you think is unwise?** → This does not mean they lack capacity — support their right to make it unless there is clear evidence of incapacity
- **Unsure whether someone has capacity for a specific decision?** → Carry out a documented capacity assessment — speak to your manager if you need support
- **Service user lacks capacity for a decision that needs to be made?** → Convene a best interest meeting involving relevant people and document the outcome

---

## Common mistakes

- Assuming someone lacks capacity because they have dementia or a learning disability — capacity must be assessed for each specific decision
- Making a best interest decision without involving the person or their family
- Confusing a best interest decision with what is most convenient for the service

---

## Who to tell

- **Your manager** — whenever a capacity assessment is needed or a best interest decision is being made
- **GP or specialist** — may need to be involved in complex capacity assessments
- **Independent Mental Capacity Advocate (IMCA)** — must be involved in certain decisions where there is no family or friend to consult

---

## Podcast notes

Work through the five principles with real examples for each. Use the scenario of a service user refusing to go to a hospital appointment to explore the difference between unwise decisions and lack of capacity. 5 minutes.
