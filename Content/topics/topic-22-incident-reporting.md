# Topic 22: Incident and accident reporting — what counts, how to record

**Theme:** Falls & incidents
**Primary format:** Text card
**Secondary format:** Quick reference card

---

## Hook

An incident report is not an admission of failure — it is the evidence that your organisation takes safety seriously and learns from what goes wrong.

---

## Key points

- An incident is any unplanned event that has caused or could have caused harm — including near misses
- Examples include falls, medication errors, aggressive behaviour, injuries, missing persons, and property damage
- Reports should be factual, objective, and written in the first person — what you saw, heard, or did
- Complete the report as soon as possible after the event while details are fresh
- Near misses must be reported too — they are early warning signs of systemic problems

---

## Quick decision prompt

- **Unsure whether something needs an incident report?** → If in doubt, complete one — over-reporting is far safer than under-reporting
- **Another staff member was involved but does not want to report?** → You still have an individual duty to report — you cannot be prevented from doing so
- **Incident involves a service user's behaviour towards a staff member?** → This still needs a report — staff safety is as important as service user safety

---

## Common mistakes

- Writing opinions or speculation rather than factual observations — "she seemed angry" not "she was angry"
- Waiting until the next day — memories fade and details are lost
- Not reporting near misses because "nothing actually happened"

---

## Who to tell

- **Your manager** — for all incidents, immediately
- **CQC** — for specified notifiable events (your manager will lead on this)
- **Local authority safeguarding team** — if the incident involves potential abuse or neglect
- **Police** — if a crime may have been committed
